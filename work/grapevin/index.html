Grapevin

Web App

Music tells a lot about a person.

That's my hypothesis: if a person's top ten current favorite songs probably tells a lot about their personality and their mood at that particular time.

Grapevin was a project to allow people to express themselves through the music they enjoy. It allowed users to easily create an interactive playlist of their favorite songs to share with their friends. Grapevin was my first project of this scale; about a hundred people decided to check it out and created a playlist.

Unfortunately, although I had plenty of ideas on how to improve the application, I couldn't maintain it. After the rise of other social music services like Spotify and Rdio, I decided to cease development of Grapevin. Since then, I've been happy to see a services like This Is My Jam and Designers.MX spring up that encapsulate lots of what I dreamt Grapevin might become.

Technology

Grapevin was built on Ruby on Rails. In fact, I learned Rails as I was working on the application and developing it served as a much superior method of learning the framework than any tutorial I could find. It also served as an two-way encouragement: sometimes I would be learning Rails to develop Grapevin and at other times I'd be developing Grapevin to learn Rails.

When a user searched for a song to add, Grapevin used the Last.fm API to grab matching songs and delivered results to the user. When the user picked one, the app then used Grooveshark API to find a related recording to embed on the user's profile page. This was one of the trickiest parts of the app: matching song data to the user's query and music then to that song data.

Design

One of my other philosophies about music is that album art is beautiful and a great complement to an auditory experience. So, I placed a great emphasis on album art in Grapevin, using them as a visual tiles representing music.

As a social application that began with no on-site community, Grapevin's key value was providing clean, interactive profile pages that could be shared elsewhere. I tried to make this easy, using Facebook and Twitter for login and placing one-click sharing buttons for these services inside the app. Every user was allowed to pick a custom username that would be used for a http://grapev.in/username URL.

Ultimately, I think Grapevin failed because it failed to provide an engaging on-site community where users could interact with one another. Given that it was my first large-scale web application and a solo project, though, I think it worked out well and I learned quite a bit from it.
